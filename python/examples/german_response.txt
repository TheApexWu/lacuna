The German Response to Versailles

Count Brockdorff-Rantzau, head of the German delegation, refused to stand when receiving the treaty terms - a deliberate act of defiance. Germany had expected negotiations based on the Fourteen Points; instead they received a Diktat - a dictated peace with no discussion.

The terms were devastating: loss of territory including Alsace-Lorraine, the Polish Corridor, and all colonies; military limitations to 100,000 troops; occupation of the Rhineland; massive reparations of undefined amount.

The Nationalversammlung in Weimar debated whether to sign. Rejection meant Allied invasion; acceptance meant national humiliation. Chancellor Scheidemann declared: "What hand would not wither that binds itself and us in these fetters?"

The treaty was signed on June 28, 1919 - exactly five years after Sarajevo. For Germans, it was a Schandfrieden (shameful peace). The seeds of Revanchismus were planted: the determination to revise the treaty and restore German honor. The Weimar Republic would never escape the stigma of those who had signed.
